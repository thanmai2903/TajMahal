You Deserve to Visit - The Taj! Once More!

Shah Jahan commissioned the Taj Mahal in 1632, shortly after the death of his wife, Mumtaz Mahal. The construction took approximately 22 years, with the mausoleum itself being completed in 1643, and the surrounding structures and gardens finished in 1653. Over 20,000 artisans and craftsmen worked on the project, with materials sourced from across India and Asia. The Taj Mahal was built as a tomb for Mumtaz Mahal, but it also served as a symbol of Shah Jahan's deep love for her. Mumtaz Mahal, whose real name was Arjumand Banu Begum, was the emperor's third wife and bore him 14 children. She was his favorite, and her death left Shah Jahan heartbroken. After the completion of the Taj Mahal, Shah Jahan intended to build a black marble mausoleum for himself across the Yamuna River. However, before he could realize this vision, he was deposed by his son Aurangzeb and spent the rest of his life under house arrest in Agra Fort. Upon his death, Shah Jahan was buried next to Mumtaz Mahal in the Taj Mahal.

Architecture of the Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal is a masterpiece of symmetry. The central dome, which is the most prominent feature of the structure, is 73 meters (240 feet) tall. Flanked by four smaller domes and four minarets, the entire structure is perfectly balanced and aligned. The minarets are slightly tilted outward, a deliberate design choice to prevent them from collapsing on the main structure in case of an earthquake. The Taj Mahal is made primarily of white marble from Makrana, Rajasthan. The marble is inlaid with precious and semi-precious stones like jade, crystal, lapis lazuli, amethyst, and turquoise, forming intricate floral patterns. The inlay work is known as "pietra dura," a technique brought to India by the Mughals. Inside the mausoleum, the cenotaphs of Mumtaz Mahal and Shah Jahan are enclosed by a finely carved marble screen, or jali. The actual graves are in a lower chamber, directly beneath the cenotaphs. The walls and ceilings of the interior are adorned with calligraphy from the Quran, intricate carvings, and decorative elements. The Taj Mahal is set in a vast Mughal garden, which is laid out in a symmetrical pattern with pathways, fountains, and reflecting pools.

Interesting Facts about the Taj Mahal

The marble of the Taj Mahal appears to change color depending on the time of day and the quality of light. It appears pinkish in the morning, milky white during the day, and golden at night under the moonlight. The calligraphy on the walls of the Taj Mahal includes verses from the Quran. The script appears uniform, but the letters are larger as they go higher, an optical illusion that makes the text appear consistent from top to bottom. The construction of the Taj Mahal is estimated to have cost about 32 million Indian rupees at the time, which would be equivalent to billions of dollars today. Materials were sourced from all over India and Asia, and it is said that a thousand elephants were used to transport building materials. Over the centuries, the Taj Mahal has faced various threats, including pollution, which has caused the marble to yellow. There are many legends associated with the Taj Mahal. One popular but unproven legend suggests that Shah Jahan had the hands of the craftsmen and architects severed after the construction so that they could never replicate such a masterpiece. The Taj Mahal remains a symbol of India's rich history, artistic achievement, and the enduring power of love. Its beauty continues to inspire visitors from around the world.
